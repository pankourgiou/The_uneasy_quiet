Part One

The Night Air

I. The Uneasy Quiet

The night air felt heavier than it should. Aeryn Mordhas stood at the edge of the town, where the last cottages gave way to sparse woodland. The roofs behind him were uneven and modest, their shapes softened by time. Yet in the dim light, they seemed… altered. Not in form. But in feeling.

Grawandor approached from the narrow road, his boots dull against the packed soil. "You've been standing here a long while," he said.

Aeryn did not turn. "Yes."

Grawandor stopped beside him. "It looks the same to me," he added after a moment.

Aeryn finally spoke. "That is what troubles me."

II. The Dream

Grawandor crossed his arms. "You had a vision."

Aeryn nodded once. "A dream."

"Those are rarely simple with you."

"No," Aeryn agreed quietly. "This one was not."

There was a long silence before he continued. "I saw Eltharion," he said. "But not as it is now."

Grawandor gave a faint, humorless smile.

"Let me guess. Burning? Broken?"

⸻

Aeryn shook his head.

"No."

That answer unsettled Grawandor more than any other.

III. A Town Transformed

"It was whole," Aeryn said. "Standing. Alive." He paused, searching for the right words. "But it had changed its… nature."

Grawandor frowned. "How?"

Aeryn turned slightly, his voice lowering. "The houses were no longer low and welcoming. They stretched upward—too narrow, too tall. Their roofs sharpened into points that pierced the sky."

Grawandor looked back at the town instinctively. "They're not like that."

"Not yet."

IV. The First Sign

Aeryn stepped forward, picking up a small stone from the ground. "In the dream," he continued, "even the simplest home had edges that felt… deliberate. As though every line was meant to guide the eye upward, away from the ground, away from comfort."

Grawandor snorted.

"You're describing pride, not danger."

—

Aeryn dropped the stone.

"No. I am describing separation."

V. The Name

"They called it something," Aeryn said.

Grawandor glanced at him. "Called what?"

Aeryn hesitated. "Goth architecture."

The words hung strangely in the air.

Grawandor frowned. "That means nothing to me."

"It didn't to me either," Aeryn replied. "Not at first."

Part Two

Living Stone

VI. Living Stone

"In the dream," Aeryn went on, "the buildings were not just structures. They were expressions." "Of what?" Grawandor asked. Aeryn answered immediately. "Of what people felt."

"You're saying the town… reshaped itself?"

"Yes."

"That's impossible."

—

Aeryn finally turned to face him fully.

"Is it?"

VII. The Emotional Drift

"The people were different," Aeryn said.

"How?"

"They moved through the streets without looking at one another. Their voices were quieter. Their laughter… rare."

Grawandor's expression darkened.

"That could happen anywhere."

—

"Yes," Aeryn agreed.

"That is why it is dangerous."

VIII. The Spread

"They dreamed it," Aeryn said.

"Dreamed what?"

"The same thing I did. Each night, more of them saw it. The towers. The arches. The endless upward reach."

Grawandor shook his head. "Dreams don't build houses."

Aeryn's voice dropped.

"No."

⸻

"But they build desires."

IX. The Subtle Corruption

Grawandor paced a few steps, clearly unsettled now. "So what—you're saying people start wanting these… structures?" "Yes." "And then what?" "They begin to rebuild."

Grawandor stopped.

"Slowly," Aeryn said. "A sharper roof here. A taller doorway there. A window narrowed." He gestured toward the town. "No one notices at first. Because each change feels… right."

X. The Meaning

Grawandor looked at him carefully.

"And what does it mean?"

—

Aeryn held his gaze.

"It means the black magicians will win."

Grawandor's jaw tightened. "You're certain?"

"Not through battle."

A long silence.

"Through transformation."

· · ·
Part Three

The Real War

XI. Fear Takes Root

Grawandor turned away, staring at the town.

"That's… worse."

"Yes," Aeryn said.

"Because you can't fight it."

—

"No," Aeryn replied softly. "You can."

Grawandor looked back sharply. "How?"

XII. The Philosophy of Form

Aeryn stepped closer. "Architecture is not just stone," he said. "It is intention made visible." Grawandor frowned. "When people feel safe," Aeryn continued, "they build wide. Open. Welcoming."

"When they don't — they build upward. Inward. Closed."

— Aeryn Mordhas

Grawandor exhaled slowly.

"So the town becomes a mirror."

—

"Yes."

XIII. The Real Battle

Grawandor's voice dropped. "Then the war isn't out there." Aeryn shook his head. He placed a hand lightly against his chest.

"It is here."

XIV — XV. A Shift Required

"If what you're saying is true… then steel won't save us."

"No."

"Then what will?"

⸻

Aeryn looked back at the town.

"Our emotions," he said.

"We must choose what shapes us."

— Aeryn Mordhas

Grawandor shook his head.

"That's a harder fight than any battlefield."

—

"Yes," Aeryn said quietly.

"It always is."

XVI — XVII. The Uncertain Dawn

They stood there together as the first hint of dawn touched the edges of Eltharion. The town looked unchanged. Still small. Still familiar. Still… itself. But Grawandor could not shake the feeling that Aeryn's dream had already begun.

"Do you think it's started?" he asked.

—

Aeryn did not answer immediately.

Then—

"Yes."

Grawandor tightened his grip on his blade. "Then we stop it."

Aeryn looked at him, not with doubt — but with something heavier. "Not alone," he said.

"Then who?"

Aeryn turned back toward the town.

"Everyone."

And as the sun rose over Eltharion, its light touched every roof, every door, every narrow path — as if reminding them what they still were.

And what they might yet become.
